## BMJ

### B1. Comparative Effectiveness of Sulfonylureas on Kidney Outcomes: A Target Trial Emulation

**Sklepinski S, Ratzki-Leewing AA, Herrin J, et al.** *BMJ Open Diabetes Research & Care.* 2026;14(3):e005775. Published 21 May 2026.

This large US target trial emulation used Optum Labs claims data (2014–2022) to compare head-to-head kidney outcomes among ~295,000 adults with type 2 diabetes initiating glimepiride, glipizide, or glyburide. Using SuperLearner propensity score-based IPTW and a new-user ITT design, the study found that glyburide was associated with significantly lower risk of CKD stage 3+ (HR 0.84 vs glimepiride; HR 0.81 vs glipizide) despite a 22–47% higher risk of severe hypoglycemia. Glipizide was modestly associated with higher kidney risk vs glimepiride. This is the first head-to-head kidney outcomes comparison of these three agents, filling a major evidence gap given the absence of postmarketing kidney outcomes trials for sulfonylureas. Falsification endpoints (pneumonia, appendicitis) showed no drug differences, supporting the credibility of the causal estimates.

### B2. Concordance and Discordance Between Randomised Clinical Trials and Emulated Target Trials (Editorial)

**Islam N, Feeney T, Kurth T.** *BMJ.* 2026;393:e007052. Published 26 May 2026.

This editorial accompanies the Wang et al. systematic review (BMJ_09) and argues that "concordance needs to be earned, not assumed." The authors emphasise that TTE quality is determined not by the analytical toolkit alone, but by alignment of research question, data quality, and design fidelity. They note that regulatory bodies including the FDA and NICE now accept real-world evidence from TTE, raising the stakes for transparent reporting. The editorial calls for empirical validation of TTE findings against available RCTs wherever possible, and for ongoing development of standards — reinforcing the role of the TARGET Statement. Full text paywalled; summarised from abstract and linked research.
