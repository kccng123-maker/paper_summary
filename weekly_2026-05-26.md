## LANCET

### L1. Personality and Mental Health as Mediators Linking Childhood Maltreatment to IPV Victimization: MR-DoC Twin Study — Pezzoli et al., Lancet Regional Health Europe 2026

This open-access study from the UK Twins Early Development Study (N = 11,342) used a Mendelian Randomization–Direction of Causation (MR-DoC) twin model to disentangle genetic confounding from causal mediation in the maltreatment–IPV pathway. After accounting for shared genetic and environmental factors, 65% of the maltreatment–IPV association was causally mediated through personality and mental health intermediaries — principally low well-being, conduct problems, and aggression — with no significant direct effect remaining (c' = 0.05, padj = 0.161). The study demonstrates that the maltreatment–IPV link is not solely attributable to shared genetic liability, implicating modifiable psychological pathways as targets for early IPV prevention. Methodologically, MR-DoC is a novel extension of twin modeling that leverages genetic instruments to strengthen causal inference.

### L2. Familial Confounding and Causal Inference in Child and Adolescent Neurodevelopment and Mental Health — Mataix-Cols et al., Lancet Child & Adolescent Health 2026

This Viewpoint, motivated by US Government claims linking prenatal paracetamol to autism, provides a clinician-targeted exposition of familial confounding — where shared genetic and environmental factors within families produce spurious associations — and its consequences for causal inference in child psychiatric epidemiology. The authors review sibling comparison, co-twin control, negative control, and Mendelian randomisation as design-based solutions, and advocate triangulation across complementary designs as best practice. The paper underscores that standard multivariable regression cannot adequately address familial confounding, and that misinterpretation of confounded associations can cause policy harm.

## BMJ

### B1. Concordance Between Target Trial Emulation and Randomised Controlled Trials: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis — Wang et al., BMJ 2026

This landmark systematic review (PROSPERO CRD42024619811) analysed 107 TTE–RCT pairs from MEDLINE, EMBASE, Scopus, PsycINFO, and Web of Science (2010–2025). Overall concordance was moderate (Pearson r = 0.59; 79% standardised difference agreement; ratio of ratios 0.96, 95% CI 0.92–1.01), improving substantially when restricted to 63 pairs with closer design emulation (r = 0.83; 87%). TTEs systematically underestimated effects for VTE and MACE outcomes (ratio of ratios 0.76 and 0.91) and overestimated effects for respiratory outcomes (1.20); claims-based data also underestimated treatment effects (0.90). The key predictors of poor concordance were imbalanced baseline population characteristics, treatment started in hospital, and lower outcome emulation quality. The findings provide the most comprehensive empirical validation of TTE methodology and identify concrete design improvements.

## JAMA

### J1. Sibling Comparisons to Account for Confounding — Hswen Y, JAMA 2026

This JAMA letter/commentary highlights the re-emergence of sibling comparison designs as essential tools for addressing unmeasured familial confounding in perinatal, neurological, psychiatric, and environmental epidemiology. Written in response to Ahlqvist et al.'s JAMA Guide to Statistics and Methods, Hswen argues that sibling designs — where discordant siblings control for shared genetic and household confounders — offer a practical design-based solution that complements propensity score methods and Mendelian randomisation. The commentary advocates triangulation across methods with distinct bias structures as the gold standard for causal inference in observational research.

## Cross-Journal Trend Notes

- **Familial confounding as a major methodological theme this week:** Two independent papers (Lancet Child & Adolescent Health viewpoint; JAMA letter) converge on sibling comparison and family-based designs as essential tools for causal inference — signalling that the field is standardising around genetically-informed confounding control.

- **MR-DoC and mediation analysis gaining ground:** The Lancet Regional Health Europe paper (Pezzoli et al.) deploys Mendelian Randomization–Direction of Causation models in a twin cohort, representing a methodological advance beyond standard MR by enabling causal mediation analysis with genetic confounding control.

- **TTE validation against RCTs now has a systematic evidence base:** The BMJ systematic review (Wang et al.) is the most comprehensive TTE–RCT concordance analysis to date (107 pairs), providing a solid empirical foundation for TTE credibility — and identifying actionable gaps (claims data underestimation; outcome measurement quality).

- **Outcome-specific bias in TTE detected:** Wang et al. found that TTE systematically underestimates VTE and MACE outcomes relative to RCTs, while overestimating respiratory outcomes — a finding that practitioners should account for when interpreting observational causal estimates in cardiology and pulmonology.

- **No NEJM TTE or causal inference activity this week:** NEJM continues to publish landmark RCTs and editorials but remains largely absent from the TTE/causal inference methodological literature, consistent with prior weeks.
