# BMJ_04 — Lived/Living Experience in DAG Construction: Applied HIV Research

**Citation:** Marziali ME, Inglis K, Frank P, Campbell W, Lambert S, Magagula P, Nicholson V, Budu M, Murray MCM, Guillemi S, Worthington C, Hogg RS. Who is the expert? A process guide to incorporate lived/living experience into directed acyclic graphs: an applied example from HIV research. *Journal of Epidemiology & Community Health (JECH/BMJ).* 2026. doi:10.1136/jech-2025-224787. Published online April 22, 2026.

---

## Brief Summary

This methods paper addresses a fundamental assumption in causal inference: that background knowledge for constructing directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) derives exclusively from academic experts. Using a community-engaged research approach in the context of HIV and ageing, the authors assembled a team including peer researchers living with HIV and co-constructed a DAG incorporating lived/living experience alongside academic domain knowledge. Qualitative analysis of DAG co-development sessions revealed that peer researchers found the process intuitive and that visual causal mapping deepened understanding and humanised the research. The paper provides a replicable process guide for integrating community expertise into the DAG construction step of epidemiologic causal inference studies, with implications for improving validity of confounder selection and causal structure specification.

## Methodology Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Causal Question as PICO / Methodological Target

| Component | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Research question** | How can lived/living experience be systematically incorporated into DAG construction for causal inference research in HIV and ageing? |
| **Population** | People living with HIV (peer researchers) and academic researchers (quantitative and qualitative epidemiologists) |
| **Intervention** | Community-engaged DAG co-development process |
| **Comparator** | Conventional academic-only expert-driven DAG construction |
| **Outcomes** | Quality and validity of DAG; peer researcher engagement and reflections; process feasibility |
| **Causal relevance** | DAG quality determines validity of confounder adjustment — errors in DAG specification propagate into biased causal estimates |

### Step 2: Data Sources

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Setting** | Community-based research team in Canada (affiliation: University of British Columbia, Simon Fraser University) |
| **Research question context** | Causal drivers of ageing outcomes in people with HIV |
| **Peer researcher panel** | Diverse group of peer researchers living with HIV, including a peer researcher-artist who illustrated the DAG |
| **Qualitative data** | Transcriptions of DAG co-development sessions |
| **Funding** | Michael Smith Health Research BC; SSHRC; Vancouver Foundation |

---

### Step 3: Process — DAG Co-Construction

**Stage 1 — Team Assembly:**
- Mixed team: quantitative epidemiologists, qualitative researchers, peer researchers with HIV, research coordinator
- Peer researchers selected for diversity of experience (demographics, treatment history, service engagement)

**Stage 2 — Background Knowledge Elicitation:**
- Academic team provided initial literature-based causal structure
- Peer researchers contributed experiential knowledge about real-world drivers of ageing with HIV (social determinants, stigma, medication adherence barriers, healthcare access)

**Stage 3 — DAG Co-Creation:**
- Collaborative sessions using plain-language descriptions of causal relationships
- Temporal ordering discussed explicitly to clarify directionality of arrows
- Peer researcher-artist created visual representation of the DAG
- Iterative refinement: epidemiologists reviewed and checked DAG structure for consistency with causal rules (no cycles, correct directionality)

**Stage 4 — Qualitative Reflection:**
- Sessions transcribed and analysed thematically
- Peer researchers asked to reflect on the process, what was intuitive or challenging, and whose expertise was being centred

### Step 5: Confounder Identification (DAG Output)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Method** | Co-constructed DAG identifying confounders, mediators, and colliders for the HIV-ageing research question |
| **Novel covariates surfaced by peer expertise** | Structural and social determinants (housing instability, food insecurity, social isolation, HIV-related stigma) not captured in routine academic literature reviews |
| **Key insight** | Peer researchers identified covariates that were causally upstream of both treatment and outcome but absent from academic DAG drafts |

### Step 6: Statistical/Analytical Methods

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|

| **DAG analysis** | Structural review by epidemiologists for DAG validity (acyclicity, directed paths) |

### Step 7: Sensitivity / Validation

- Iterative peer review of DAG by epidemiologists to check for structural errors introduced during co-construction
- Qualitative member-checking: peer researchers reviewed transcripts for accuracy
- Multiple co-development sessions allowing revision of causal structure

## Key Results

| Finding | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Feasibility** | DAG co-construction with peer researchers is feasible and acceptable |
| **Intuitive engagement** | Peer researchers found DAG drawing intuitive; visual representation of causal relationships stimulated active discussion |
| **Humanisation** | Seeing the web of covariates resonated as a "reflection of a person's life journey" — increased emotional and intellectual engagement |
| **Knowledge diversity** | Incorporating diverse perspectives meant "upholding unique expertise and not only platforming academic expertise" |
| **Structural benefit** | Peer researchers surfaced social and structural determinants (stigma, housing, access) not in the academic draft DAG |

## Strengths

- Addresses a real methodological gap: DAG construction is typically opaque and academic-dominated, yet is foundational to valid causal inference
- Process guide is explicit and replicable — other research teams can follow the described steps
- Qualitative component enables critical reflection on the process itself (meta-methodology)
- Community-engaged approach aligns with principles of patient and public involvement (PPI) in research
- Applied example grounded in a real, ongoing HIV ageing study (not hypothetical)
- Peer researcher-artist participation produces a more accessible, shareable DAG artefact

## Limitations

- No quantitative validation of the resulting DAG (e.g., d-separation testing, comparison of estimates with/without peer-derived covariates)
- Single disease context (HIV ageing in Canada) — generalisability of process to other diseases/settings requires further evaluation
- Resource-intensive: requires dedicated time, community relationships, and qualitative researchers alongside epidemiologists
- Peer researchers may underrepresent the most marginalised groups (selection bias in community research teams)
- No formal assessment of how much the peer-derived DAG changes the resulting causal estimates relative to an academic-only DAG
