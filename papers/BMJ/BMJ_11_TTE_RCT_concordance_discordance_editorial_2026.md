# Concordance and Discordance Between Randomised Clinical Trials and Emulated Target Trials

**Citation:** Islam N, Feeney T, Kurth T. Concordance and discordance between randomised clinical trials and emulated target trials. *BMJ.* 2026;393:e007052. doi:10.1136/bmj-2026-007052. Published 26 May 2026.

## Brief Summary

This editorial commentary by Islam, Feeney, and Kurth accompanies the systematic review by Wang et al. (BMJ 2026) on concordance between target trial emulations (TTEs) and randomised controlled trials (RCTs). The authors argue that "concordance needs to be earned, not assumed" — that TTE results should be validated against RCTs where possible, not simply accepted on methodological grounds. They note that with the increasing availability of large electronic health records, well-developed causal inference methods, and regulatory acceptance by agencies such as the FDA and NICE, TTE has become a complementary evidence source. However, concordance is not guaranteed, and the quality of observational causal inference depends not on the analytical toolkit alone but on the alignment of research question, data quality, and study design. This editorial reinforces the TARGET Statement (Cashin et al., JAMA 2025) call for transparent reporting and signals a maturing field grappling with when TTE findings can and cannot be trusted.

## Methodology Step-by-Step

*This is an editorial/methodology commentary, not an original research study. No primary data were analyzed.*

### Step 1: Causal Question

The editorial addresses the conceptual question: *Under what conditions do target trial emulations produce treatment effect estimates concordant with randomised controlled trials, and what are the implications when they diverge?*

### Step 2: Data Sources

- No primary data. Draws on the Wang et al. systematic review (BMJ 2026) and prior literature on TTE methodology.

### Step 3: Key Methodological Arguments

| Argument | Detail |
|---|---|
| **TTE framework** | Requires explicit specification of estimand, eligibility criteria, time zero, and treatment strategy |
| **Sources of bias addressed** | Confounding by indication, immortal time bias, selection bias |
| **Concordance is not guaranteed** | Agreement between TTE and RCT depends on research question alignment, data quality, and design fidelity |
| **Regulatory acceptance** | FDA and NICE now accept real-world evidence from TTE for some decisions |
| **Role of electronic health records** | Large EHRs enable population-representative TTE that RCTs cannot achieve |

## Key Points

| Theme | Editorial Position |
|---|---|
| RCTs vs TTE | RCTs remain gold standard but are expensive, slow, and conducted in selective populations |
| When TTE succeeds | When design mimics RCT protocol, data quality is high, and confounders are well-measured |
| When TTE diverges from RCT | May reflect unmeasured confounding, population differences, or design misalignment |
| Recommendation | Concordance should be demonstrated empirically, not assumed; TTE results should be triangulated with other evidence |
