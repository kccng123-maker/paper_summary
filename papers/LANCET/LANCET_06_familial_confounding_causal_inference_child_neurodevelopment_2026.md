# LANCET_06 — Familial Confounding and Causal Inference in Child and Adolescent Neurodevelopment and Mental Health

**Citation:** Mataix-Cols D, Pol-Fuster J, Fernández de la Cruz L, Lichtenstein P, D'Onofrio BM, Brander G, et al. Familial confounding and causal inference in child and adolescent neurodevelopment and mental health. *The Lancet Child & Adolescent Health.* 2026. doi:10.1016/S2352-4642(26)00045-3. Published online March 24, 2026.

*Note: Full text unavailable (paywalled). Summary based on abstract/viewpoint summary only.*

---

## Brief Summary

This Viewpoint, motivated by recent US Government claims linking prenatal paracetamol exposure to autism, argues that familial confounding — where shared genetic or environmental factors within families produce spurious associations — is a pervasive and underappreciated bias in child and adolescent psychiatric/neurodevelopmental epidemiology. The authors, drawing on population-based sibling and family-based study designs from Scandinavian registries and UK cohorts, illustrate how failing to account for familial confounding can generate false causal conclusions from observational research and misguide policy. They outline study designs to address familial confounding (sibling comparison, co-twin control, negative control, Mendelian randomisation), their trade-offs, and emphasise triangulation across complementary designs as best practice for strengthening causal inference in this field.

## Methodology Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Causal Question as Viewpoint Framing

| Element | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Methodological Viewpoint / narrative review |
| **Central question** | What is familial confounding, how does it affect causal inference in child neurodevelopment/mental health research, and how can it be addressed? |
| **Target audience** | Clinicians working with children/young people with neurodevelopmental and mental health conditions |
| **Motivating example** | US Government linking prenatal paracetamol to autism (based on observational data ignoring familial confounding) |

### Step 2: Key Concepts Covered (from abstract/viewpoint structure)

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| **Familial confounding** | Shared genetic or environmental factors within families that confound exposure–outcome associations |
| **Gene–environment correlation** | Genetic influences on likelihood of being exposed to environmental risk factors |
| **Spurious association** | Phenotypic correlation between prenatal exposures and child outcomes driven by familial factors, not causal effects |
| **Study designs to address confounding** | Sibling comparison, co-twin control, negative control outcomes, Mendelian randomisation, direction-of-causation models |

### Step 3: Illustrative Examples (from abstract and references)

- Prenatal paracetamol use and autism/ADHD risk (Ahlqvist et al., JAMA 2024 — sibling-controlled study)
- Maternal infection during pregnancy and autism (Brynge et al., Lancet Psychiatry 2022 — negative control + sibling comparison)
- Prenatal antidepressant use and preterm birth/ASD (Sujan et al., JAMA 2017 — co-twin control)
- Severe childhood infections and OCD (Pol-Fuster et al., BMJ Mental Health 2024 — sibling comparison)

### Step 4: Study Designs Outlined for Addressing Familial Confounding

| Design | Principle | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sibling comparison** | Compares discordant siblings; controls for shared family factors | Controls genetic + shared env confounding | Requires large registries; reduces power; unmeasured within-family differences |
| **Co-twin control** | MZ twin pairs discordant on exposure | Controls genetic + shared env confounding maximally | Rare exposure discordance; generalisability |
| **Negative control outcomes** | Outcomes biologically unrelated to exposure but sharing same confounders | Detects residual confounding | Requires valid negative control selection |
| **Mendelian randomisation** | Uses genetic instruments as IVs | Controls measured + unmeasured confounding | Pleiotropy; weak instruments; population-specific |
| **Triangulation** | Convergence across multiple designs | Strengthens causal inference when consistent | Designs may share some biases |

### Step 5: Confounder Adjustment Framework

- Emphasises that standard multivariable regression cannot fully adjust for familial confounding
- Recommends design-based (rather than purely analysis-based) confounding control
- Triangulation across designs with different bias structures as key recommendation

### Step 6: Policy Implications

- Misinterpretation of confounded associations can lead to harmful policy (e.g., advising pregnant women to avoid paracetamol without evidence of causation)
- Calls for systematic use of family-based designs in child neurodevelopmental epidemiology
- "Association does not equal causation" framed as a clinical communication priority for paediatric clinicians

### Step 7: Key Recommendations

- Use family-based or genetically informed designs (sibling, co-twin, MR) as the standard in child psychiatric epidemiology
- Report familial confounding analyses alongside any observational association study in this field
- Triangulate across designs with distinct bias structures before drawing causal conclusions
- Engage with clinical audiences to prevent misinterpretation of observational findings

## Strengths

- Timely and policy-relevant: directly addresses misinformation from misinterpreted observational data
- Clear, clinician-targeted exposition of a complex methodological concept
- Draws on high-quality Scandinavian registry studies with family-based designs
- Promotes triangulation — a principled, increasingly recognised approach to causal inference
- Lancet Child & Adolescent Health platform ensures reach to paediatric clinicians

## Limitations

- Viewpoint format — not a systematic review; examples selectively chosen
- Full text unavailable for detailed methodological assessment
- Does not address all sources of confounding beyond familial factors
- Triangulation guidance remains qualitative without formal frameworks for evidence synthesis
