# Physical Activity and Biological Age Measured by DNA Methylation Clocks: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

**Citation:** Shan J, Tay JH, Wang W, Tan R, Joshi R, Maier AB, et al. Physical activity and biological age measured by DNA methylation clocks: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *The Lancet Healthy Longevity.* 2026. doi:10.1016/S2666-7568(26)00019-X. Published online April 29, 2026. [Open Access] PROSPERO: CRD42024499021.

---

## Brief Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis synthesised evidence from 44 studies (145,465 participants) on the association between physical activity and biological age measured by DNA methylation (DNAm) epigenetic clocks. Across studies, higher physical activity was generally associated with lower DNAm age. Meta-analysis of seven cross-sectional studies found that each 1 SD higher physical activity (MET-min/week) was associated with 0.03 SD lower Horvath epigenetic age acceleration (EAA; β=−0.03, 95% CI −0.05 to −0.01) and 0.09 SD lower GrimAge EAA (β=−0.09, 95% CI −0.12 to −0.05). No significant associations were found for Hannum EAA or PhenoAge EAA. The authors emphasise that the predominance of cross-sectional designs limits causal inference and call for longitudinal trials with objectively measured physical activity to establish whether exercise can causally decelerate biological ageing.

## Methodology

### Step 1: Causal Question / Review Question as PICO Specification

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Population** | Adults ≥18 years (mean/median age), any health status except disease-specific populations without controls |
| **Exposure** | Physical activity (any type, any measure — self-report or objective); quantified as MET-min/week in meta-analysis |
| **Comparator** | Lower physical activity (dose-response association) |
| **Outcome** | Primary: DNAm age, epigenetic age acceleration (EAA), or epigenetic age deviation as measured by DNAm clocks (Horvath, Hannum, PhenoAge, GrimAge, DunedinPACE, others) |
| **Study designs** | Observational (cross-sectional, cohort), interventional (RCTs, exercise trials) |
| **Causal inference note** | Review explicitly acknowledges that cross-sectional evidence cannot establish causality; calls for RCTs and longitudinal studies |

### Step 2: Data Sources / Search Strategy

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Databases searched** | Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, PubMed, Ovid, Scopus, Web of Science |
| **Search period** | January 1, 2011 – June 6, 2025 |
| **N studies identified** | 34,437 initial results |
| **N studies included (SR)** | 44 studies |
| **N participants (SR)** | 145,465 (62,887 female [43.2%], 82,578 male [56.8%]) |
| **Age range** | Mean/median 24.1–78.5 years across studies |
| **N studies in meta-analysis** | 7 cross-sectional studies (harmonised MET-min/week available) |
| **PROSPERO registration** | CRD42024499021 |

### Step 3: Eligibility Criteria

**Inclusion:**
- Peer-reviewed, published in English
- Study population: mean/median age ≥18 years
- Investigated association between any DNAm clock and physical activity in humans
- Reported relevant effect estimates or data for standardised β calculation

**Exclusion:**
- Disease-specific populations without healthy controls (e.g., cancer survivors only, without comparison group)
- Non-human studies
- Studies not reporting DNAm clock measures

### Step 4: Exposure Classification (Physical Activity Measures)

- **Diverse measures across studies:** accelerometry (objectively measured), self-report questionnaires, MET-min/week, step counts, structured exercise protocols
- **Meta-analysis harmonisation:** Standardised β coefficients derived from reported estimates or re-calculated from reported data
- **Physical activity unit:** 1 SD increase in MET-min/week used as the exposure unit for meta-analysis

### Step 5: DNAm Clock Classification and Outcome

| Clock Generation | Clocks Included | Training Outcome |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **1st generation** | Horvath1, Hannum, Horvath2 | Chronological age |
| **2nd generation** | PhenoAge, GrimAge1, GrimAge2, DNAmFit, DNAmTL | Biomarker composites, plasma proteins, telomere length |
| **3rd generation** | DunedinPoAm, DunedinPACE | Longitudinal health outcomes (pace of ageing) |

- **EAA (Epigenetic Age Acceleration):** DNAm age − chronological age (residual from regression); positive = biologically older than expected
- **DNAm age deviation:** Alternative metric used in some studies

### Step 6: Statistical Analysis

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Meta-analytic model** | Random-effects meta-analysis |
| **Effect measure** | Standardised β coefficient (SD change in EAA per 1 SD change in MET-min/week) |
| **Heterogeneity** | I² statistic reported |
| **Publication bias** | Funnel plots; Egger's test |
| **Influence analysis** | Leave-one-out sensitivity analysis |
| **Risk of bias** | Adapted Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (observational studies); Cochrane Risk of Bias tool (RCTs) |
| **GRADE** | Not explicitly reported |

### Step 7: Sensitivity Analyses

1. **Leave-one-out influence analysis** — sequentially excluded each study to assess whether any single study drives pooled estimates
2. **Publication bias analysis** — funnel plots and Egger's test to detect small-study effects
3. **Clock-stratified analyses** — separate pooled estimates for each DNAm clock (Horvath, Hannum, PhenoAge, GrimAge)
4. **Objective vs self-reported physical activity** — assessed as potential source of heterogeneity

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive systematic search** across 6 major databases covering all DNAm clocks since 2011
2. **PROSPERO pre-registration** — reduces outcome reporting bias
3. **Three generations of DNAm clocks** analysed separately — enables comparison across clock methodologies
4. **Harmonised physical activity metric (MET-min/week)** for meta-analysis — improves comparability across heterogeneous studies
5. **Large aggregate sample** (145,465 participants across 44 studies)
6. **Publication bias and influence analyses** included
7. **Transparent about causal inference limitations** — explicitly states that evidence cannot establish causality
8. **Open access** — Lancet Healthy Longevity; full methods and data accessible

## Limitations

1. **Predominantly cross-sectional studies** — only 7 cross-sectional studies contributed to the meta-analysis; temporal ordering of exposure and outcome cannot be established
2. **Heterogeneous physical activity measures** — mix of self-report and objective measures; MET-min/week harmonisation required assumptions
3. **Limited RCT evidence** — exercise interventional trials with DNAm clock outcomes are sparse and small
4. **Different DNAm clock generations not equally powered** — 3rd generation clocks (DunedinPACE) underrepresented
5. **No dose-response curve** — meta-analysis provides linear estimate but cannot characterise non-linear effects or threshold
6. **Causal inference cannot be established** — authors acknowledge this prominently; reverse causation possible (healthier/younger-biologically people may exercise more)
7. **Publication bias** — small positive studies more likely published; funnel plots tested but asymmetry may remain
8. **No adjustment for physical activity type** (aerobic vs resistance vs combined) — differential effects by modality not examined
9. **Age and sex heterogeneity** not fully explored — biological ageing trajectories differ substantially by sex and life stage
